# Prioritized Experience Replay

Uniform experience replay treats all transitions equally, but some transitions are far more informative than others. Prioritized Experience Replay (PER) samples transitions in proportion to their **TD error** -- the "surprise" factor -- leading to significantly faster and better learning.

## Motivation: Not All Experiences Are Equal

### The Problem with Uniform Sampling

With a replay buffer of 1 million transitions and minibatch size 32, each transition is sampled on average $\frac{32}{1{,}000{,}000} = 0.0032\%$ per step. Many of these transitions are "boring" -- the agent already predicts them well and learns nothing from replaying them.

Meanwhile, some transitions are "surprising":
- Transitions where the agent discovered a new reward
- Transitions where the agent's prediction was very wrong
- Transitions at critical decision points

These high-information transitions are sampled at the same rate as uninformative ones. This is highly suboptimal.

### The Key Insight

The **TD error** measures how "surprising" a transition is:

$$
\delta_i = \left| r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s', a'; \theta^-) - Q(s, a; \theta) \right|
$$

A large $|\delta_i|$ means the agent's prediction was far off -- there is much to learn. A small $|\delta_i|$ means the agent already predicts this transition well.

## Proportional Prioritization

### Sampling Probability

Instead of sampling uniformly, PER samples transition $i$ with probability:

$$
P(i) = \frac{p_i^\alpha}{\sum_k p_k^\alpha}
$$

where:
- $p_i$ is the **priority** of transition $i$
- $\alpha$ controls how much prioritization is used:
  - $\alpha = 0$: uniform sampling (no prioritization)
  - $\alpha = 1$: full prioritization (pure greedy)

### Setting Priorities

**Proportional method:** Priority is based on the absolute TD error:

$$
p_i = |\delta_i| + \epsilon
$$

where $\epsilon > 0$ is a small constant that ensures all transitions have nonzero probability of being sampled (typically $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$).

**Rank-based method:** Priority is based on the rank of the TD error:

$$
p_i = \frac{1}{\text{rank}(i)}
$$

where $\text{rank}(i)$ is the position of transition $i$ when sorted by $|\delta_i|$ in descending order.

### Proportional vs Rank-Based

| Property | Proportional | Rank-Based |
|---|---|---|
| Sensitivity to outliers | High (one huge TD error dominates) | Low (uses rank, not magnitude) |
| Implementation | Easier | Requires sorting |
| Distribution shape | Depends on TD error distribution | Always follows a power law |
| Performance | Slightly better in practice | More robust |

## Importance Sampling Correction

### The Bias Problem

Prioritized sampling introduces **bias**: transitions with high priority are overrepresented in the training data. This means the gradient estimates are biased -- we are not optimizing the true loss over the uniform data distribution.

### Correcting with Importance Sampling Weights

To correct this bias, we weight each sample by the inverse of its sampling probability:

$$
w_i = \left( \frac{1}{N \cdot P(i)} \right)^\beta
$$

where:
- $N$ is the replay buffer size
- $P(i)$ is the sampling probability
- $\beta \in [0, 1]$ controls the amount of correction:
  - $\beta = 0$: no correction (fully biased)
  - $\beta = 1$: full correction (unbiased)

The weights are **normalized** so the maximum weight is 1:

$$
w_i \leftarrow \frac{w_i}{\max_j w_j}
$$

### Annealing $\beta$

In practice, $\beta$ is annealed from a small value to 1 over the course of training:
- Early in training: $\beta$ is small, allowing biased but fast learning
- Late in training: $\beta \rightarrow 1$, the estimates become unbiased (important for convergence)

### The Corrected Loss

The loss for a minibatch becomes:

$$
L(\theta) = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{j=1}^{B} w_j \cdot \left( y_j - Q_\theta(s_j, a_j) \right)^2
$$

High-priority samples get lower weights (because they are sampled more often), and low-priority samples get higher weights.

## The Full PER Algorithm

```
Algorithm: DQN with Prioritized Experience Replay

Initialize replay memory D to capacity N with priorities
Initialize Q with random weights theta, target Q_hat with theta^- = theta

For episode = 1 to M:
  For t = 1 to T:
    Select action a_t (epsilon-greedy from Q_theta)
    Execute a_t, observe r_t, s_{t+1}
    Store (s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1}) in D with maximal priority p_t = max_{i<t} p_i

    Sample minibatch of B transitions from D using P(i) = p_i^alpha / sum_k p_k^alpha
    Compute importance sampling weights w_j = (1/(N*P(j)))^beta / max_i w_i

    Compute TD errors: delta_j = r_j + gamma * max_a' Q_hat(s'_j, a'; theta^-) - Q_theta(s_j, a_j)
    Update priorities: p_j = |delta_j| + epsilon

    Perform gradient descent on: sum_j w_j * delta_j^2
    Periodically update theta^- <- theta
  End For
End For
```

**Note:** New transitions are added with **maximum priority** so they are guaranteed to be sampled at least once.

## Results: Prioritized Replay Performance

### Atari Benchmark (Schaul et al., 2016)

| Metric | DQN Baseline | DQN Rank-Based | DDQN Baseline | DDQN Rank-Based | DDQN Proportional |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Median | 48% | 106% | 111% | 113% | **128%** |
| Mean | 122% | 355% | 418% | 454% | **551%** |
| > baseline | -- | 41 | -- | 38 | **42** |
| > human | 15 | 25 | 30 | 33 | **33** |
| # games | 49 | 49 | 57 | 57 | 57 |

(Percentages relative to human performance)

**Key findings:**
- PER leads to **much faster learning** -- the learning curve rises more steeply
- Both proportional and rank-based methods improve significantly over uniform
- The combination of Double DQN + proportional PER achieves the best overall results
- PER is especially beneficial in games with sparse or delayed rewards

## Worked Example: Prioritized Sampling

Consider a buffer with 5 transitions and $\alpha = 1$:

| Transition | TD Error $|\delta_i|$ | Priority $p_i = |\delta_i| + 0.001$ | $P(i)$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 5.0 | 5.001 | 50.0% |
| B | 2.0 | 2.001 | 20.0% |
| C | 0.1 | 0.101 | 1.0% |
| D | 1.5 | 1.501 | 15.0% |
| E | 1.4 | 1.401 | 14.0% |

**With uniform sampling:** Each transition has $P(i) = 20\%$.

**With prioritized sampling:** Transition A (highest surprise) has 50% chance of being sampled. Transition C (already well-predicted) has only 1% chance.

**Importance sampling weight for transition A** (with $\beta = 1$):
$$
w_A = \left(\frac{1}{5 \times 0.50}\right)^1 = 0.4
$$

This down-weights the gradient from over-sampled transition A, correcting the bias.

## Practical Considerations

### Computational Efficiency

Naive PER requires updating all priorities after each gradient step (expensive). In practice:
- Only update priorities for transitions **in the current minibatch**
- Use a **sum-tree** data structure for efficient $O(\log N)$ sampling and priority updates
- A sum-tree is a binary tree where each parent stores the sum of its children's priorities

### Stale Priorities

Priorities can become **stale**: a transition might have had a high TD error when first observed, but after learning, the error might be low. The priorities are only refreshed when a transition is sampled again. This is acceptable in practice because:
- New transitions start with maximum priority
- Frequently sampled transitions get updated regularly
- The staleness effect diminishes with large buffers

## Key Takeaway

> Prioritized Experience Replay samples transitions proportionally to their absolute TD error -- "surprising" transitions are replayed more often. This dramatically accelerates learning by focusing training on the most informative experiences. Importance sampling weights correct the bias introduced by non-uniform sampling, and are annealed from partial to full correction during training. PER is compatible with and complementary to both Double DQN and Dueling DQN.

**Reference:** "Prioritized Experience Replay", Schaul et al. (2016).
