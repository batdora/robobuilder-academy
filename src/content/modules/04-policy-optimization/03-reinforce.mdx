# REINFORCE and Variance Reduction

We derived the policy gradient. Now we turn it into a practical algorithm: **REINFORCE** (Williams, 1992). We will also tackle its biggest weakness -- high variance -- through **temporal structure** and **baseline subtraction**.

## The Vanilla Policy Gradient (REINFORCE)

From the previous lesson, our gradient estimate is:

$$
\hat{g} = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \left(\sum_{t=0}^{H} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(u_t^{(i)} | s_t^{(i)})\right) R(\tau^{(i)})
$$

The **REINFORCE** algorithm simply uses this gradient in a loop:

### Algorithm: REINFORCE (Monte Carlo Policy Gradient)

> 1. Initialize policy parameters $\theta$
> 2. **for** iteration $= 1, 2, \ldots$ **do**
> 3. &emsp; Collect $m$ trajectories $\tau^{(1)}, \ldots, \tau^{(m)}$ by executing $\pi_\theta$
> 4. &emsp; For each trajectory, compute $R(\tau^{(i)}) = \sum_{t=0}^{H} R(s_t^{(i)}, u_t^{(i)})$
> 5. &emsp; Compute gradient estimate: $\hat{g} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m} \nabla_\theta \log P(\tau^{(i)}; \theta) \cdot R(\tau^{(i)})$
> 6. &emsp; Update: $\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \hat{g}$
> 7. **end for**

## The High Variance Problem

While unbiased, the vanilla gradient estimate has **extremely high variance**. Why?

1. **Entire trajectory return:** Each action's gradient is weighted by the return of the *entire* trajectory. But an action at time $t$ cannot affect rewards that already happened at times $0, 1, \ldots, t-1$.

2. **All-positive or all-negative returns:** If all returns are positive (common in many tasks), the gradient tries to increase the probability of *all* trajectories -- just more for better ones and less for worse ones. This wastes gradient signal.

3. **Single-sample Q estimate:** We estimate $Q^\pi(s, u) = \mathbb{E}[r_0 + r_1 + r_2 + \cdots | s_0 = s, a_0 = a]$ from a single rollout, which has very high variance.

## Temporal Structure (Causality)

### The Key Insight

Consider the gradient estimate expanded:

$$
\hat{g} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\left(\sum_{t=0}^{H-1} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(u_t^{(i)}|s_t^{(i)})\right)\left(\sum_{t'=0}^{H-1} R(s_{t'}^{(i)}, u_{t'}^{(i)})\right)
$$

We can split the total return for each log-probability term. The reward at time step $t'$ can be split into:

- Past rewards: $\sum_{k=0}^{t-1} R(s_k^{(i)}, u_k^{(i)})$ -- happened before action $u_t$
- Future rewards: $\sum_{k=t}^{H-1} R(s_k^{(i)}, u_k^{(i)})$ -- happened after action $u_t$

The past rewards **do not depend on action $u_t^{(i)}$**, so including them only adds noise. We can remove them!

### The Improved Estimator

After exploiting temporal structure, we get:

$$
\hat{g} = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{t=0}^{H-1} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(u_t^{(i)} | s_t^{(i)}) \left(\sum_{k=t}^{H-1} R(s_k^{(i)}, u_k^{(i)})\right)
$$

Each action's gradient is now weighted only by the **future return** (reward-to-go) from that time step onward, not by past rewards that the action could not have influenced.

## Baseline Subtraction

### Motivation

Even with temporal structure, variance is still high. The idea of a **baseline** is to subtract a constant (or state-dependent function) from the return:

$$
\hat{g} = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \nabla_\theta \log P(\tau^{(i)}; \theta) \left(R(\tau^{(i)}) - b\right)
$$

### Proof That the Baseline Does Not Introduce Bias

We need to show $\mathbb{E}[\nabla_\theta \log P(\tau; \theta) \cdot b] = 0$ for any baseline $b$ that does not depend on the action in the log-probability:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\nabla_\theta \log P(\tau; \theta) \cdot b] = \sum_\tau P(\tau; \theta) \nabla_\theta \log P(\tau; \theta) \cdot b
$$

$$
= \sum_\tau P(\tau; \theta) \frac{\nabla_\theta P(\tau; \theta)}{P(\tau; \theta)} \cdot b
$$

$$
= \sum_\tau \nabla_\theta P(\tau; \theta) \cdot b
$$

$$
= \nabla_\theta \left(\sum_\tau P(\tau; \theta)\right) \cdot b = \nabla_\theta(1) \cdot b = 0
$$

Since $\sum_\tau P(\tau; \theta) = 1$ always. Therefore subtracting *any* baseline $b$ that does not depend on the action keeps the estimator **unbiased** while potentially reducing variance.

## Baseline Choices

### 1. Constant Baseline

The simplest choice: $b = \mathbb{E}[R(\tau)] \approx \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m} R(\tau^{(i)})$

Just the average return across your batch of trajectories.

### 2. Optimal Constant Baseline

The baseline that minimizes variance of the gradient estimate:

$$
b^* = \frac{\sum_i \left(\nabla_\theta \log P(\tau^{(i)}; \theta)\right)^2 R(\tau^{(i)})}{\sum_i \left(\nabla_\theta \log P(\tau^{(i)}; \theta)\right)^2}
$$

This weights returns by the squared magnitude of their score functions.

### 3. Time-Dependent Baseline

A different baseline for each time step:

$$
b_t = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{k=t}^{H-1} R(s_k^{(i)}, u_k^{(i)})
$$

### 4. State-Dependent Baseline: The Value Function

The best practical choice -- use the **state value function** as baseline:

$$
b(s_t) = \mathbb{E}\left[r_t + r_{t+1} + r_{t+2} + \cdots + r_{H-1}\right] = V^\pi(s_t)
$$

**Intuition:** Increase the log-probability of action proportionally to *how much better its returns are than the expected return* under the current policy. This is exactly the **advantage**:

$$
A^\pi(s, a) = Q^\pi(s, a) - V^\pi(s)
$$

## The Full Gradient Estimate with Baseline

Combining temporal structure and the value function baseline:

$$
\hat{g} = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{t=0}^{H-1} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(u_t^{(i)} | s_t^{(i)}) \left(\sum_{k=t}^{H-1} R(s_k^{(i)}, u_k^{(i)}) - V^\pi(s_t^{(i)})\right)
$$

The term $\sum_{k=t}^{H-1} R(s_k^{(i)}, u_k^{(i)}) - V^\pi(s_t^{(i)})$ is a **sample-based estimate of the advantage** $\hat{A}_t$.

## The Vanilla Policy Gradient Algorithm

This is the complete practical algorithm from (Schulman et al.):

> **Algorithm: "Vanilla" Policy Gradient**
>
> Initialize policy parameter $\theta$, baseline $b$
>
> **for** iteration $= 1, 2, \ldots$ **do**
> 1. Collect a set of trajectories by executing the current policy
> 2. At each timestep in each trajectory, compute:
>    - the return $R_t = \sum_{t'=t}^{T-1} \gamma^{t'-t} r_{t'}$
>    - the advantage estimate $\hat{A}_t = R_t - b(s_t)$
> 3. Re-fit the baseline, by minimizing $\|b(s_t) - R_t\|^2$, summed over all trajectories and timesteps
> 4. Update the policy, using a policy gradient estimate $\hat{g}$, which is a sum of terms $\nabla_\theta \log \pi(a_t | s_t, \theta) \hat{A}_t$
>
> **end for**

Note the introduction of the **discount factor** $\gamma$ in the return computation $R_t = \sum_{t'=t}^{T-1}\gamma^{t'-t}r_{t'}$. This further reduces variance by down-weighting distant future rewards.

## Estimating the Value Function Baseline

How do we estimate $V^\pi(s)$ for the baseline? Two approaches:

### Monte Carlo Estimation

1. Initialize $V_{\phi_0}^\pi$ (a neural network with parameters $\phi$)
2. Collect trajectories $\tau_1, \ldots, \tau_m$
3. Regress against empirical returns:

$$
\phi_{i+1} \leftarrow \arg\min_\phi \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{t=0}^{H-1}\left(V_\phi^\pi(s_t^{(i)}) - \sum_{k=t}^{H-1} R(s_k^{(i)}, u_k^{(i)})\right)^2
$$

### Bootstrap Estimation

Use the Bellman equation:

$$
V^\pi(s) = \sum_u \pi(u|s) \sum_{s'} P(s'|s, u)[R(s, u, s') + \gamma V^\pi(s')]
$$

1. Initialize $V_{\phi_0}^\pi$
2. Collect data $\{s, u, s', r\}$
3. Fitted V iteration:

$$
\phi_{i+1} \leftarrow \min_\phi \sum_{(s, u, s', r)} \|r + V_{\phi_i}^\pi(s') - V_\phi(s)\|_2^2 + \lambda \|\phi - \phi_i\|_2^2
$$

The regularization term $\lambda\|\phi - \phi_i\|_2^2$ prevents the value function from changing too drastically between iterations.

## Variance Reduction by Discounting

Recall:

$$
Q^\pi(s, u) = \mathbb{E}[r_0 + r_1 + r_2 + \cdots | s_0 = s, a_0 = a]
$$

Estimating this from a single rollout has very high variance because distant rewards have high uncertainty. We introduce the **discount factor** $\gamma$ as a hyperparameter:

$$
Q^{\pi, \gamma}(s, u) = \mathbb{E}[r_0 + \gamma r_1 + \gamma^2 r_2 + \cdots | s_0 = s, u_0 = u]
$$

With $\gamma < 1$, distant rewards are down-weighted, reducing variance at the cost of some bias.

## Worked Example: REINFORCE with Baseline

**Setup:** One state $s$, two actions $a_1, a_2$. Gaussian policy on a 1D continuous action. We collect 3 trajectories:

| Trajectory | Action $u$ | Return $R(\tau)$ |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | 0.5 | +10 |
| 2 | -0.3 | +2 |
| 3 | 0.8 | +12 |

**Without baseline ($b = 0$):** All returns are positive, so we push up the probability of ALL actions. The gradient signal is noisy.

**With constant baseline $b = \bar{R} = 8$:**

| Trajectory | $R(\tau) - b$ | Effect |
|-----------|--------------|--------|
| 1 | +2 | Slightly increase $P(u=0.5)$ |
| 2 | -6 | Decrease $P(u=-0.3)$ |
| 3 | +4 | Increase $P(u=0.8)$ |

Now the gradient has clear positive and negative signals, much more informative!

## Key Takeaway

> REINFORCE computes policy gradients from sampled trajectories. The raw estimator has high variance, which we reduce through (1) **temporal structure** (only future rewards), (2) **baseline subtraction** (especially $V^\pi(s)$), and (3) **discounting**. The Vanilla Policy Gradient algorithm combines all these ideas into a practical method that forms the foundation for all advanced policy gradient algorithms.
