# Natural Policy Gradient

Vanilla policy gradient uses the standard gradient $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$, which moves in the direction of steepest ascent in **parameter space**. But parameter space and **policy space** are different: a small change in $\theta$ might cause a huge change in policy behavior, or vice versa. The **Natural Policy Gradient** fixes this by taking the steepest ascent in **policy distribution space**.

## The Problem with Vanilla Gradients

### Step Size Sensitivity in RL vs Supervised Learning

As highlighted by Pieter Abbeel, step sizes matter much more in RL than in supervised learning:

**Supervised learning:** If you step too far, the next update will correct for it. The dataset is fixed and does not change based on your parameters.

**Reinforcement learning:** If you step too far, you get a **terrible policy**. The next mini-batch is collected under this terrible policy! You collect bad data, which leads to worse updates, leading to even worse data. Recovery is difficult -- you may need to go back and shrink the step size.

This is why we need to be very careful about how far we step in policy space.

### Gradient is Only a First-Order Approximation

The gradient $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$ is a local linear approximation. It is only accurate for small steps. But how small is "small"? In parameter space, a step of size $\epsilon$ might:

- Change the policy drastically (if parameters are very sensitive in that region)
- Barely change the policy at all (if parameters are in a flat region)

We need a step-size criterion that relates to the actual change in **policy behavior**.

## Measuring Policy Change: KL Divergence

To measure how much the policy changes, we use the **KL divergence** between the old and new policy:

$$
D_{KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\theta + \delta\theta}) = \sum_s d^\pi(s) \sum_a \pi_\theta(a|s) \log \frac{\pi_\theta(a|s)}{\pi_{\theta+\delta\theta}(a|s)}
$$

where $d^\pi(s)$ is the state visitation distribution under $\pi$.

### Evaluating KL Divergence Over Trajectories

Recall:

$$
P(\tau; \theta) = P(s_0) \prod_{t=0}^{H-1} \pi_\theta(u_t | s_t) P(s_{t+1} | s_t, u_t)
$$

The KL divergence between trajectory distributions:

$$
KL(P(\tau;\theta) \| P(\tau;\theta+\delta\theta)) = \sum_\tau P(\tau;\theta) \log \frac{P(\tau;\theta)}{P(\tau;\theta+\delta\theta)}
$$

Substituting the trajectory probability:

$$
= \sum_\tau P(\tau;\theta) \log \frac{P(s_0)\prod_{t=0}^{H-1}\pi_\theta(u_t|s_t)P(s_{t+1}|s_t,u_t)}{P(s_0)\prod_{t=0}^{H-1}\pi_{\theta+\delta\theta}(u_t|s_t)P(s_{t+1}|s_t,u_t)}
$$

**The dynamics cancel out!** $P(s_0)$ cancels, and $P(s_{t+1}|s_t,u_t)$ cancels:

$$
= \sum_\tau P(\tau;\theta) \log \frac{\prod_{t=0}^{H-1}\pi_\theta(u_t|s_t)}{\prod_{t=0}^{H-1}\pi_{\theta+\delta\theta}(u_t|s_t)}
$$

$$
\approx \frac{1}{M}\sum_{(s,u) \in \text{rollouts}} \log\frac{\pi_\theta(u|s)}{\pi_{\theta+\delta\theta}(u|s)}
$$

This is model-free and can be estimated from samples.

## The Fisher Information Matrix

For small $\delta\theta$, the KL divergence can be approximated using a second-order Taylor expansion:

$$
D_{KL}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\theta+\delta\theta}) \approx \frac{1}{2}\delta\theta^\top F(\theta) \delta\theta
$$

where $F(\theta)$ is the **Fisher Information Matrix (FIM)**:

$$
F(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\left[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s) \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s)^\top\right]
$$

The FIM captures the **local curvature of the policy distribution**. It tells us how sensitive the policy distribution is to changes in each direction of parameter space.

**Properties of the FIM:**
- Symmetric and positive semi-definite
- Independent of the reward function (it only depends on the policy)
- Defines a **Riemannian metric** on the parameter space

## The Natural Gradient

### Standard Gradient Descent

Standard gradient ascent solves:

$$
\max_{\delta\theta} \; \nabla_\theta J(\theta)^\top \delta\theta \quad \text{subject to} \quad \|\delta\theta\|_2^2 \leq \epsilon
$$

Solution: $\delta\theta \propto \nabla_\theta J(\theta)$ (step in the Euclidean gradient direction)

### Natural Gradient Descent

Natural gradient ascent instead constrains the change in **policy space** (KL divergence):

$$
\max_{\delta\theta} \; \nabla_\theta J(\theta)^\top \delta\theta \quad \text{subject to} \quad \frac{1}{2}\delta\theta^\top F(\theta) \delta\theta \leq \epsilon
$$

Solution (using Lagrange multipliers):

$$
\delta\theta \propto F(\theta)^{-1} \nabla_\theta J(\theta)
$$

This is the **natural gradient**: the standard gradient pre-multiplied by the inverse Fisher Information Matrix.

### Why the Natural Gradient is Better

The natural gradient accounts for the geometry of the policy distribution:

1. **Parameterization invariant:** If you reparameterize the policy (e.g., going from $\theta$ to $\psi = g(\theta)$), the standard gradient changes, but the natural gradient gives the same update in policy space.

2. **Appropriate step sizes:** In directions where small parameter changes cause large policy changes, the FIM is large, so $F^{-1}$ shrinks the step. In directions where large parameter changes barely affect the policy, the FIM is small, so $F^{-1}$ enlarges the step.

3. **Better conditioning:** Neural network loss landscapes are often ill-conditioned. The natural gradient implicitly preconditions the optimization.

## Computing the Natural Gradient

The main challenge is computing $F^{-1}g$ where $g = \nabla_\theta J(\theta)$.

### Direct Computation

For a policy with $n$ parameters, $F$ is an $n \times n$ matrix. Inverting it costs $O(n^3)$, which is prohibitive for neural networks with millions of parameters.

### Conjugate Gradient Method

Instead of computing $F^{-1}$ explicitly, we can solve $Fx = g$ for $x = F^{-1}g$ using the **conjugate gradient (CG)** method. CG only requires computing **matrix-vector products** $Fv$, which can be done efficiently:

$$
Fv = \mathbb{E}\left[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s) \left(\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a|s)^\top v\right)\right]
$$

This is called a **Fisher-vector product** and can be computed using automatic differentiation without ever forming the full matrix $F$.

## Monotonic Improvement Theory

A key theoretical result (Kakade & Langford, 2002; Schulman et al., 2015) provides conditions for **guaranteed policy improvement**:

$$
J(\pi_{\text{new}}) \geq J(\pi_{\text{old}}) + \mathbb{E}_{s \sim d^{\pi_{\text{old}}}}\left[\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi_{\text{new}}}\left[A^{\pi_{\text{old}}}(s, a)\right]\right] - C \cdot D_{KL}^{\max}(\pi_{\text{old}} \| \pi_{\text{new}})
$$

where $C$ is a constant depending on the discount factor and reward bounds, and $D_{KL}^{\max}$ is the maximum KL divergence over all states.

**Interpretation:** The new policy is guaranteed to be at least as good as the old policy, as long as:
1. The expected advantage under the new policy is positive (we choose better actions)
2. The KL divergence penalty is small (we don't change too much)

This is the theoretical foundation for TRPO (next lesson).

## Step Sizing Approaches

Given the natural gradient direction $\tilde{g} = F^{-1}g$, how do we choose the step size?

### Line Search

Simple but expensive: try different step sizes $\alpha$ along $\tilde{g}$ and evaluate each:

$$
\theta_{\text{new}} = \theta + \alpha \tilde{g}
$$

Pick the $\alpha$ that gives the best improvement. Requires multiple evaluations.

### Trust Region

Directly constrain the KL divergence:

$$
\alpha = \sqrt{\frac{2\epsilon}{\tilde{g}^\top F \tilde{g}}}
$$

where $\epsilon$ is the maximum allowed KL divergence. This gives the largest step in the natural gradient direction that stays within the trust region.

## Worked Example: Natural vs Standard Gradient

**Setup:** 2D parameter space. $\nabla_\theta J = [1, 1]^\top$. Fisher Information Matrix:

$$
F = \begin{bmatrix} 100 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

**Standard gradient step:** $\delta\theta \propto [1, 1]$ -- equal step in both dimensions.

**Natural gradient step:**

$$
\delta\theta \propto F^{-1}\nabla_\theta J = \begin{bmatrix} 1/100 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.01 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

The natural gradient takes a tiny step in dimension 1 (where the policy is very sensitive, $F_{11} = 100$) and a full step in dimension 2 (where the policy is less sensitive, $F_{22} = 1$). This prevents catastrophic policy changes in sensitive directions.

## Key Takeaway

> The **Natural Policy Gradient** replaces the standard gradient with $F^{-1}\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$, where $F$ is the Fisher Information Matrix. This ensures that each update causes a controlled change in the policy distribution (measured by KL divergence) rather than in raw parameter space. The natural gradient is parameterization-invariant and leads to more stable optimization. Combined with the monotonic improvement theory, it sets the stage for TRPO.
